Content Information

The My Thuan Bridge (Vietnamese: Cầu Mỹ Thuận) is a cable-stayed bridge over the Mekong river, connecting Cai Be District of Tien Giang Province with Vinh Long City of Vĩnh Long Province in Vietnam. It was developed in a joint venture between the governments of Australia and Vietnam. The bridge was the largest overseas assistance project undertaken by the Australian government costing A$91 million.

History

My Thuan ferry was formerly located on National Highway 1, connecting two provinces of Tien Giang and Vinh Long, with two north and south coasts about 1 km apart. Since 2000, this ferry terminal was stopped when My Thuan Bridge was put into use.

Before the French came to Vietnam, there was a wharf in My Thuan village (Kien Dang district, Dinh Tuong province, Hoa Hung commune, Cai Be district, Tien Giang province). The ferry terminal was built around 1910 when there was a car on the road. The south of the ferry is now located in My Thuan hamlet, Tan Hoi commune, Vinh Long city, and 9 km west of the city center.

My Thuan ferry plays an important role in the transportation of road in the South West.

In 1950, the United States had intended to provide capital to the Saigon government to build the bridge but failed. In the mid-1960s, Nippon Koei (Japan) completed the project design and was selected, but the project was canceled due to financial difficulties. According to the Australian Government's AusAID program, the My Thuan Bridge project has a total investment of AUD $ 90.86 million (approximately VND2,000 billion), of which the Australian Government grants 66% The Vietnamese side is 34% successful. Vietnam is preparing capital to continue building My Thuan 2 bridge with dedicated roads for the metro with a total investment of more than 7,000 billion.
